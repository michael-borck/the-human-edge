Afternoon workshop: worked examples

A model answer for each RetailFlow initiative, across all three sprints.

Worked examples for the afternoon RetailFlow sprints — scope, stakeholders & human-in-the-loop, roadmap & the go/no-go.

These are worked examples for the afternoon’s three sprints — one set per funded RetailFlow initiative. Use them to compare against your own group’s design, and to see how the day’s tools (the trust tool, the five differences, Scale/Pivot/Kill) play out on a real scope. Read them after your group has worked through its own sprints; the value is in the comparison, not in copying the answer.

Each set mirrors the worksheets: Sprint 1 scopes the objective against reality (data, readiness, trust-tool split, a defined “good enough”). Sprint 2 maps stakeholders and designs the human-in-the-loop checkpoints. Sprint 3 builds the roadmap with gates, a risk register, an assumptions register, and the gate-1 go/no-go call.

How to read the gate-1 calls

All four initiatives land on Pivot at gate 1 — not by coincidence, but because each genuinely rests on a shaky, load-bearing assumption at the first evaluation gate:

  • Chatbot — the returns policy exists in four disagreeing copies, and a retired 14-day window is still live in the support macros.
  • Pricing — there is no clean competitor feed, so the engine degrades to inventory-only; the backtest won’t hold live.
  • Inventory — stock-on-hand can’t be fully trusted, on a fragile, end-of-support inventory estate.
  • Fraud — today’s false-block rate is unmeasurable, so the “baseline to beat” is unknown.

So don’t read these for the verdict. The interesting contrast is in the reason. Each answer carries its conditional Scale and Kill path, because a defensible Pivot is one that turns into Scale if the shaky assumption clears — and into Kill if it breaks. The judgement is in defending the call under challenge.

The four initiatives

Expand an initiative, then expand a sprint inside it.

Customer-service chatbot · $450K

Grounded in: board funding memo (28 Jan 2026), Priya’s data-readiness note (11 Feb 2026), returns policy v4.1, ConverseAI vendor proposal, CS team email thread (15 Jun 2026), org chart, draft AI principles..

Sprint 1 — scope it against reality
  • Scoped objective: deploy a chatbot to deflect routine factual queries (order status) and cut average email response time from 26h toward <4h (board memo). In: factual queries answerable from data. Human-checked or routed (for now): returns (30-day change-of-mind; sale/clearance final; faulty goods covered under the ACL regardless of window) and sizing/fit (tacit — denim runs small, “king” = Australian king — lives in agents’ heads). Out: complaints/escalations, phone, social DMs.
  • Readiness: data – best of the four (~214K Zendesk tickets over 2.3 yrs + Shopify live-chat, clean and labelled by query type), but ground truth is poor (the RAG work is the critical path). Organisation – Tom’s CS team owns review + escalation design (operational owner, per org chart); sponsor Emma carries the board promise (~6-month build, board committed; the board privately expects “live in a quarter”); pilot already in flight. Build/buy/host – buy the tool, not the trust. The ConverseAI pitch is a trap: its “97.8% accuracy” is on the vendor’s own RetailBench corpus, not our policies; per-conversation pricing ($0.42) is uncapped at volume; and Singapore/US-East hosting breaches our draft data-sovereignty principle (Principle 2). Buy the platform; the grounding is ours.
  • Data (from Priya): best transcript foundation of the four — but “good foundation” ≠ “knows your policy.” Returns policy exists in ≥4 disagreeing copies (website footer, in-store card, 2022 intranet PDF, Zendesk macros); the macro library still quotes a 14-day window retired in 2024 (actual: 30 days). Sizing knowledge and discretionary return exceptions are unwritten. Real work: consolidate to one ratified canonical policy source, owned jointly with Tom, before the bot goes beyond pilot.
  • Trust-tool split: order status → lean in, with a human glance (it’s customer-facing); returns/sizing → human in the loop — a wrong policy in writing is a reputational + consumer-law exposure (the board flagged this itself).
  • “Good enough”: deflect ≥55% of inbound (the board commitment; push toward 60%); wrong-answer rate <2% on routine factual, but 0% tolerance on returns/policy — the bot must refuse and route to a human if unsure.
Sprint 2 — stakeholders & human-in-the-loop
  • Stakeholder plan: Emma (MD/sponsor, High/High) – needs the board promise kept; fears reputational damage → agree “good enough” gates + phased rollout, no big-bang. David (CFO) – needs ROI on $450K against the $1.1M cost-avoidance run-rate he signed off; fears runaway cost → tie spend to gates, show deflection savings. Tom (CS manager, operational owner, High interest) – his team is automated first; fears job loss + looking bad → position as augmentation (board committed no role cuts), staff the checkpoints with CS. Lisa Nguyen (GM CX, Tom’s manager) ratified the v4.1 returns policy with Tom — she is the policy authority and the escalation owner for CX risk.
  • HITL checkpoints: Returns/policy answers → senior CS reviewer checks before send (or the bot refuses + routes). Evaluator: an experienced senior rep — the one who already makes the discretionary calls. Builds capability (the deliberate build-checkpoint — juniors learn what “right” looks like here) + consumes. High-confidence routine answers → QA-sample (~5%), human glance. Evaluator: CS lead. Mostly consumes.
  • The non-negotiable: returns-policy answers must be human-verified or refused — a wrong policy in writing (the bot quoting “14 days” instead of 30) is the single biggest risk, and per Raj’s team email it is already happening.
Sprint 3 — roadmap, risk & the go/no-go
  • Roadmap with gates: Pilot (narrow) – order-status + returns, shadow mode (bot drafts, human sends), 2 weeks. Gate: ≥70% of drafts usable with <3% wrong. Evaluate – add sizing; measure deflection + wrong-rate. Gate: deflection ≥55% (board), wrong <2%, policy-wrong = 0. Scale/Pivot/Kill → Production with monitoring.
  • Risk register (top): confident-wrong returns policy (AI-specific: non-determinism + verification) – Med/High – mitigate: RAG + human checkpoint, bot refuses if unsure. Already live, not theoretical: Raj’s email (15 Jun) records the bot telling a customer “14 days” (order #RF-880214) — the grounding gap in action. Demo-gap (AI-specific): the demo handles the easy 80% – mitigate: gate on the angry-customer edge cases. Vendor trap (non-AI but real): offshore hosting + uncapped per-conversation cost – mitigate: require Aus-region hosting per Principle 2; forecast/cap usage.
  • Assumptions register (load-bearing): (1) the policy docs are current & complete → shaky (4 disagreeing copies, retired 14-day macro still live); (2) the bot routes to a human when unsure → holding (designed in, Sprint 2); (3) CS has capacity to review every policy answer → shaky. Three of three shaky-or-tenuous → exactly why gate-1 is Pivot, not Scale.
  • Gate-1 call: PIVOT if the returns wrong-rate is too high at gate 1 – narrow scope to order-status only until the policy RAG matures. Rationale (traces to assumption 1, shaky): the cost of a wrong policy at scale — consumer-law + reputational — exceeds the cost of narrowing scope. Scale only if policy-wrong = 0 holds.
  • DDCD crisis (the screenshot incident): a customer screenshots the bot quoting “14 days” returns. Diagnose = technical grounding gap + ethical/consumer-law exposure; Decide = pause the bot on returns, force-route to human, retire the stale 14-day Zendesk macro, re-ground on v4.1; Communicate = to the customer (apologise + honour the 30-day return), Tom’s team, Emma/board; Document = root cause (grounded on a stale macro) + fix + ongoing policy-wrong monitoring.
Dynamic pricing · $850K
Sprint 1 — scope it against reality
  • Scoped objective: deliver human-approved, demand-led price recommendations on a narrow pilot (slow-moving online fashion), not autonomous repricing. In: within-guardrail markdown recommendations, online-first where the data is clean. Human-approved (for now): any change >20% in one step, hero/top-200 SKUs, essential-homewares increases, anything below cost. Out: autonomous mode, store prices (POS not reconciled), essential homewares, brand-hero lines.
  • Readiness: data – poor. No clean competitor feed (manual ad-hoc checks, weeks stale; a matched feed is a separate $80–120K/yr project not in the $850K). Store demand (legacy POS, ~65% of revenue) is coarse, batch-loaded, ~14% SKU mismatched to online. Organisation – sponsor David (CFO), delivery lead Priya, tech Marcus, brand Lisa; governance policy v4.2 (owner Sarah/COO) is a hard constraint. Build/buy/host – buy the engine + build the feed/reconciliation + a governance wrapper; never accept the vendor’s autonomous default.
  • Data (from Priya): competitor feed is the critical gap; online/store won’t join cleanly; and backtest ≠ live — discount the vendor’s +3.8pt claim “heavily.” Real work = feed acquisition + SKU reconciliation + a capped live pilot. 12+ months, not “this year.”
  • Trust-tool split: pricing sits in the Precise / high-stakes danger corner. Small within-band markdowns on slow stock → Average, can automate inside guardrails. Anything touching a threshold, a hero line, or a margin floor → human in the loop.
  • “Good enough”: metric = full-price sell-through (target 61%→67%) / margin lift on pilot SKUs vs a control. Threshold = a measurable, attributable lift. Maximum error = zero tolerance on governance breaches (below-cost, >20% unapproved step, >7-day swing, >5% channel divergence, gouging). A wrong price is public.
Sprint 2 — stakeholders & human-in-the-loop
  • Stakeholder plan: David (CFO/sponsor, High/High) – needs ROI on $850K + the “this year” margin lift; fears blowout → tie spend to gates, surface the $80–120K feed cost up front, frame the pilot as de-risking the backtest. Emma (MD, High/High) – owns the board’s “live this year” promise; fears a gouging headline → reframe “this year” as a branded-safe pilot, not full rollout, phased. Marcus (CIO, High/Med) – wants speed, “competitors aren’t waiting” → channel his urgency into the real blocker (data engineering: feed + reconciliation), not the engine. Priya (delivery lead, High/High) – the honest 12-month timeline → make her the gatekeeper. Sarah (COO, High power/High interest) – owns governance; will slow it, rightly → embed her policy as hard guardrails, not a negotiation. Lisa (GM CX) – brand perception + hero-SKU sign-off → in early on hero rules.
  • HITL checkpoints: any change >20% in a step, hero/top-200 SKUs, homewares increases, or below-cost → merchandise manager / COO / CFO approve before it goes live (policy §1–2). Evaluator: experienced category buyer / merchandise manager. Builds capability (deliberate build-checkpoint — juniors learn pricing judgement, not just rubber-stamp) + consumes. Small within-band markdowns → QA-sample (~5%), human glance. Evaluator: merchandise manager. Mostly consumes. Brand-perception breaches (cadence, channel divergence, gouging) → automated daily check + weekly CX review. Evaluator: Lisa’s team. Consumes.
  • The non-negotiable: no price goes live automatically above the thresholds — >20% step, hero line, or below-cost all require human sign-off as a hard block, not an alert. A wrong price is public and instant; “autonomous by default” is a non-starter here.
Sprint 3 — roadmap, risk & the go/no-go
  • Roadmap with gates: Phase 0 – Data foundation (parallel): acquire + match the competitor feed; reconcile online/store SKU keys. ~3–4 months. Gate 0: feed live, ≥95% SKU match, reconciliation done — no engine contract before this clears. Phase 1 – Pilot: one slow-moving online fashion category, engine in recommendation-only mode, every change human-approved, guardrails ON (not the vendor default), shadow run ~6–8 weeks vs a control. Gate 1: measurable margin lift vs control, 0 governance breaches, backtest-vs-live delta understood. Phase 2 – Expand categories, still human-approved above threshold; add stores once POS reconciled. Phase 3 – Trust-banded autonomy: only ≤small within-band changes ever automated; full autonomous mode is never reached.
  • Risk register (top): autonomous wrong price live (AI-specific: non-determinism + verification) – Med impact / High – mitigate: guardrails ON by default, hard-block above threshold. Demo-gap / backtest overstatement (AI-specific): the +3.8pt is replayed against own history, can’t see competitor/customer reaction – mitigate: live capped pilot vs control. Missing competitor feed (AI-specific + data-discovered): engine falls back to inventory-and-demand-only, mis-prices the 65%-of-revenue store base – mitigate: Phase-0 feed project, online-first pilot. Brand/gouging exposure (high-stakes, amplified by speed): 7-day swing, >5% channel divergence – mitigate: governance as hard guardrails + CX review.
  • Assumptions register (load-bearing): (1) a clean competitor feed can be acquired + matched → shaky (Priya: critical gap, separate $80–120K/yr project, not in budget); (2) online/store demand can be reconciled → shaky (legacy POS, ~14% SKU mismatch, batch); (3) governance guardrails can be enforced as hard blocks in the vendor engine → holding (available in console, but disabled by default — must be turned ON); (4) the backtested margin lift holds live → shaky/broken (Priya: discount heavily); (5) the board accepts “this year” = a pilot, not full rollout → shaky (the central tension). Two core assumptions shaky/broken → why gate-1 is PIVOT, not Scale.
  • Gate-1 call: PIVOT — ship only human-approved recommendations on a narrow online category, not autonomous pricing. Traceable to assumption #1 (competitor feed — shaky/broken: the engine degrades to inventory-only without it) and #4 (the backtest won’t hold in live). The cost of an autonomous wrong price at scale — a gouging story or margin loss across thousands of SKUs — far exceeds the cost of staying human-approved. Scale only if #1 and #4 are proven holding at the pilot; if the feed genuinely can’t be sourced, KILL (inventory-only repricing against a 65%-store blind spot has no defensible value).
  • DDCD crisis example: a repricer hiking an essential homewares line +11% in a week (breaching the +10%/quarter cap) the morning of a competitor stockout — instant “gouging” headline; only the human checkpoint catches it, the engine sees only opportunity.
Inventory optimisation · $1.1M
Sprint 1 — scope it against reality
  • Scoped objective: deliver AI demand-forecasting and advisory replenishment for one category (homewares) across pilot stores — prove value, then scale. In: single-category forecast + daily replenishment suggestions ranked by stockout risk; automate the suggestion. Human-approved (for now): any actual reorder/write-back; the seasonal buy forecast. Out: every other category, real-time store stock (the system can’t give it), auto-commit/auto-replenish, network-wide rollout, POS replacement.
  • Readiness: data – online (Shopify) clean & API-accessible; store POS (RetailMaster, ~12 yrs) is nightly flat-file only — a 24-hr lag; StockTrak inventory (~14 yrs) carries manual adjustments and ~7% variance vs counts; ~22% of SKUs need mapping/cleansing; no shared product key; supplier lead times live in spreadsheets/email. Organisation – Priya accountable, Marcus owns integration, Sarah holds ops; Sarah’s own policy keeps pilot suggestions advisory only. Build/buy/host – buy the forecasting platform ($420K licensing); the real work is the $390K integration, which is a data-quality program, not a connector.
  • Data (from Priya): product-master reconciliation + SKU mapping must precede modelling; trustworthy stock-on-hand (~93% today) must improve; reconcile StockTrak ↔︎ POS ↔︎ Shopify. Realistic 9–12 months, pilot ~3 months — not the 6 quoted.
  • Trust-tool split: aggregate demand signal + stockout-risk ranking → Average → automate the suggestion. The actual commit/write-back to a fragile, end-of-support system, and the seasonal buy (large, irreversible $) → Precise → human approves. Per-store/regional forecasts where a “right in aggregate” demo can be wrong-on-a-store → human check.
  • “Good enough”: single metric = store-manager acceptance rate of pilot suggestions (the trust proxy Sarah bakes in); proposed gate ≥70% accepted, with pilot-line stockout ≤6% (board target) and zero nil-stock regressions vs baseline. Unacceptable: a confident forecast that drives a wrong reorder in a specific store — pile-up or nil-stock — even when the network average looks right (worst case: worsening the DC’s chronic homewares-candle nil-stock in regional stores).
Sprint 2 — stakeholders & human-in-the-loop
  • Stakeholder plan: Emma (MD/sponsor, High/High) – owns the “whole-network gains” board promise; fears a visible failure on the biggest spend → reframe as prove-then-scale (board already endorsed), milestone-gated budget, set the 9–12 month expectation now. David (CFO, High/High) – needs ROI/certainty on $1.1M; fears the underestimated integration blows the budget → first tranche only $340K, release the rest against gates. Marcus (CIO, High/High) – owns integration; impatient for transformation but knows the legacy pain best → he’s the pilot’s ally; agree integration is a data-quality program; protect his 3 data engineers (binding constraint across all four initiatives) with sequencing. Priya (Data, High/Med-High, accountable) – pushes the single-category pilot + realism → adopt her roadmap. Sarah (COO, High/Med) – store managers ignore forecasts they don’t trust; fears steamrolling frontline → recommendation-a-manager-approves, not auto-replenish; she is the gatekeeper of trust. Tom/Lisa: keep informed (stockouts hurt CX).
  • HITL checkpoints: (1) Every replenishment suggestion approved by the store manager before commit — evaluator: the store manager (knows what right looks like for their store); builds capability (managers stay fluent in reorder judgement while the system learns — the apprenticeship point) and consumes. (2) SKU-mapping / stock-on-hand sign-off before the model trains — evaluator: Priya’s data engineers + Raj Patel (the one person who knows StockTrak’s schema — key-person risk); builds. (3) Seasonal-buy forecast review — evaluator: buying team + Priya; mostly consumes (large, irreversible $).
  • The non-negotiable: no auto-commit to StockTrak — every suggestion is advisory, a store manager approves before write-back. It bounds blast radius on the broadest-scope initiative and protects a fragile, out-of-support system. Both Marcus’s note and Sarah’s policy say exactly this.
Sprint 3 — roadmap, risk & the go/no-go
  • Roadmap with gates: Phase 0 — Integration groundwork (~6 wks): StockTrak nightly replica + Shopify feed in staging; SKU-master cleanse for the homewares slice; cross-train Raj’s knowledge. Gate 0: trustworthy, reconciled homewares data + key-person risk mitigated before any modelling. Phase 1 — Pilot (homewares, ~3 mo): advisory daily suggestions ranked by stockout risk, manager approves. Gate 1 (go/no-go): forecast acceptance ≥ gate, pilot stockout ≤6%, no nil-stock regressions, nightly pipeline stable. Phase 2 — Scale: waves of categories/stores; move stable lines toward auto-suggest (still no auto-commit while StockTrak is EOL). Phase 3 (FY27+): POS replacement unlocks real-time + auto-commit.
  • Risk register (top): Right-in-aggregate / wrong-on-a-store (AI-specific: demo gap + non-determinism) — Med/High — mitigate: pilot, per-store validation, gate on worst store not network average. Integration under-scoped (AI-specific: data is discovered, not specified) — High/High — mitigate: $390K line, treat as data-quality program, Phase 0 first. Auto-write to EOL StockTrak — Med/High — mitigate: advisory-only, no auto-commit. Key-person risk (Raj) — Med/High — mitigate: cross-train, document, Gate 0. Manager distrust — Med/High — mitigate: manager-approves model + Sarah’s change management.
  • Assumptions register (load-bearing): (1) homewares SKU-cleanse delivers trustworthy stock-on-hand → shaky (no shared product key; “reconciliation is a project in itself”). (2) nightly batch / 24-hr lag is tolerable → holding (designed in). (3) StockTrak replica + Shopify feed stand up in ~6 wks → shaky (EOL, undocumented). (4) managers accept the suggestions (trust) → shaky (Sarah: they ignore what they don’t trust). (5) Raj stays available through Phase 0 → shaky. (6) StockTrak stays operational (EOL mid-2024) → shaky. (7) board’s prove-then-scale framing holds → holding (explicitly endorsed). Five of seven load-bearing assumptions sit on the data/integration layer — the most underestimated part of the budget.
  • Gate-1 call: PIVOT by default; SCALE only if all data-and-trust gates clear; KILL only if the core extraction assumption breaks. Because the load-bearing integration assumptions (#1, #3, #6) are shaky, gate-1 leans Pivot — hold scope at homewares and fix the data layer — even if the demo looks good, rather than Scale on a forecast built on stock-on-hand we can’t fully trust. Scale only if homewares data is clean, the pipeline is stable, Raj risk is mitigated, and managers are accepting suggestions with zero nil-stock regressions. Kill only if trustworthy data genuinely can’t be extracted from the EOL estate — at which point inventory waits for the FY27 POS replacement, it isn’t force-fit.
  • DDCD crisis line: a confident forecast under-orders the homewares-candle line for regional stores — turning the pilot’s flagship category into the exact stockout the board funded this to fix.
Fraud detection · $650K
Sprint 1 — scope it against reality
  • Readiness: data – Shopify Plus order data (value, device/IP, billing–shipping mismatch, tenure, payment method) is clean and adequate to feed a vendor, but we don’t have enough labelled fraud history to build our own (Priya’s reason buy wins). Legacy POS is poor, but in-store card-present is the smaller exposure – not blocking. organisation – the manual rule-based review is straining, inconsistent online vs store, and we cannot measure our own false-block rate today (the baseline we must beat). Sponsor is Marcus (CIO); Priya owns threshold/oversight design; Tom is consulted on anything customer-facing. build/buy/host – buy, don’t build: this is a vendor-integration + oversight project, not a data-science build. Fraud is an always-on control → it sits with Risk/Ops + vendor, NOT in Priya’s analytics backlog.
  • Scoped objective: cut card-not-present chargebacks ≥40% and fraudulent returns/refund abuse ≥30% within 12 months of go-live. In: online card-not-present detection via vendor + threshold tuning + human review of flagged-before-block. Human-checked (for now): the borderline/flagged review queue. Out: in-store card-present fraud (smaller exposure, poor data); building a proprietary model; any auto-decline without review.
  • Data (from Priya): data is “good enough for a vendor” – and that’s the point. The real data work isn’t ingestion, it’s instrumenting the false-positive rate so we can finally see it, and demanding the vendor’s FP rate at our threshold on data like ours, not a 99.2% headline benchmarked on other retailers.
  • Trust-tool split: clear-clean majority (low risk) → lean in / auto-approve at checkout (speed). Borderline/flagged (~8% middle) → PRECISE: human in the loop before any customer is blocked. The threshold is a business trade-off owned by us (Finance + CS in the room), never the vendor’s default.
  • “Good enough”: no single metric – a paired trade-off. Primary: chargeback reduction ≥40%. Constraint: false-block rate must be measurable and must not exceed today’s manual baseline; the vendor’s ~1-in-6-of-flagged default (≈1.3% of all online orders wrongly interfered with) is the number to tune DOWN. Unacceptable: blocking a legit customer (esp. long-standing/wholesale) without review – “a wrongly accused customer rarely returns.”
Sprint 2 — stakeholders & human-in-the-loop
  • Stakeholder plan: David (CFO, High/High) – owns the $3.2M FY25 loss (up from $2.4M) + the $650K; wants certainty on the 40%/30% cuts; fears runaway per-transaction fees ($0.11/order scales with growth) and a loss-cutter that blocks good customers (the board’s hard constraint) → tie spend to gates; show chargeback $ saved vs per-order cost; Finance sits in the threshold room. Priya (Data, Med/High) – owns buy-not-build + oversight design; needs her team NOT running it; fears a black box we can’t oversee → she sets the measurement spec + vendor-oversight framework, explicitly not the ongoing model. Tom (CS, Med/High) – his queue absorbs every wrongly-blocked customer (3+ escalations/month already, one furious wholesale customer); fears looking bad + the 4-hr SLA already slipping in sales → staff the review checkpoint with his experienced agents; position as augmentation; he signs off anything customer-facing. Marcus (CIO/sponsor) – tempted by the 4-week vendor go-live; gate him on the demo-to-production reality.
  • HITL checkpoints: Flagged-before-block (borderline) → experienced CS/risk reviewer checks and contacts the customer before any decline – “security verification” framing, never the word “fraud” (policy). Evaluator: senior reviewer (the evaluator’s advantage – they know real-fraud vs nervous-good-customer; a junior becomes a conduit who waves it through). Builds capability (deliberate judgement work grows tomorrow’s evaluators) and consumes. High-confidence-fraud auto-decline, if used → QA-sample + audit, human can override; mostly consumes. Threshold-setting reviews (Finance + CS + Priya) → human judgement owns the trade-off; builds institutional capability.
  • The non-negotiable: no customer is auto-declined on the vendor’s score alone – flagged transactions get human review (or a safe release-and-monitor path) before block. A wrongly blocked legit customer is the single biggest risk (revenue + reputation), the board’s explicit hard constraint, and the AI-principles “high-impact action = HITL” rule.
Sprint 3 — roadmap, risk & the go/no-go
  • Roadmap with gates: Phase 0 Baseline & diligence (wks 1–4): instrument today’s manual false-block rate (can’t beat what we can’t measure); vendor POC on RetailFlow’s own data; demand FP rate at our threshold. Gate: FP-on-our-data < now-measured baseline; contract clause locking threshold ownership to us (not the Enterprise-tier hostage). Phase 1 Pilot/shadow (online card-not-present only): vendor runs alongside manual rules; Finance+CS+Priya tune; flagged → human review before block. Gate (end-Q2 board review): chargebacks trending ≥40% cut AND false-block ≤ baseline AND review queue within SLA. Phase 2 Live online + extend to returns/refund-abuse. Gate: hold both at scale. Phase 3 in-store (out for now). Scale/Pivot/Kill → production.
  • Risk register (top): Demo-to-production gap (AI-specific: demo on THEIR data) – the 99.2%/62% are portfolio benchmarks on other retailers’ data, say little about our patterns – Med/High – mitigate: POC on our data, FP-at-our-threshold before signing. False-block at scale (AI-specific: non-determinism + trade-off + verification) – ~1-in-6 of ~8% flagged ≈ 1.3% of all online orders wrongly interfered with; reputational + lost sales; we can’t measure it yet – High/High – mitigate: human-before-block, instrument the metric, tune down. Black-box/lock-in (AI-specific: can’t verify why) – proprietary model, can’t explain a decline; bespoke thresholds cost extra – Med/High – mitigate: contract for threshold ownership + explainability + exit. Per-order cost creep (not AI-specific) – $0.11/order scales with growth – Med/Med – mitigate: fee cap, gate review.
  • Assumptions register (load-bearing): (1) vendor FP rate on RetailFlow data ≈ portfolio benchmark → shaky (benchmarked on others’ data); (2) today’s manual false-block rate is a beatable baseline → shaky/unknown (we don’t measure it yet – foundational); (3) CS has capacity + expertise to review every flagged-before-block within SLA → shaky (4-hr target already slips; reviewer notes inconsistent); (4) threshold config is ours, not vendor-locked → shaky (standard = vendor default; bespoke = Enterprise extra). Four-of-four shaky → why gate-1 is Pivot, not Scale.
  • Gate-1 call: PIVOT if the false-block rate on our data exceeds the now-measured baseline OR the review queue blows the SLA at end-Q2. Action: tune thresholds up (more conservative blocking), make human-review-before-block mandatory on all borderline (no auto-decline) until the vendor FP rate is demonstrably ≤ baseline on our data. Rationale: the board’s hard constraint is customer experience – a loss-cutter that blocks good customers “will not be judged a success”; assumption (2) is the core load-bearing one and it is unverifiable-or-broken at gate-1, but fixable by tuning + HITL → Pivot, not Kill. Scale only if (1)+(2)+(3) all hold.
  • Crisis example: the wholesale-customer blow-up (finance email) is the template – a high-value, long-standing customer wrongly blocked and furious; playbook = human-before-block + assume-good-faith “security verification” comms + a fast release path.
NoteThese are model answers, not the only defensible ones

A different group, reading the same RetailFlow documents and interviewing the same stakeholders, can arrive at a different — equally defensible — design. The point of the afternoon is the reasoning you bring to scope, checkpoints and the go/no-go, not matching a fixed key. Compare your group’s calls against these and ask: where would we defend a different call, and why?
