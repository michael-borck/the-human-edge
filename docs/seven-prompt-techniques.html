Seven prompt techniques to try

Seven ready-to-use patterns that put your judgement in charge of the output.

Seven prompting techniques — reverse prompting, pros and cons, stepwise thinking, role play, debating, practice, and the expert panel — with ready-to-use prompts and discipline-specific examples.

These seven prompting techniques are reusable patterns for getting more out of AI on your own work — drafting a policy, weighing a decision, rehearsing a difficult conversation, pressure-testing a recommendation. Each one turns the AI from an answer machine into a sparring partner, a coach, or a panel of experts. They’re not about clever prompting for its own sake; they’re about keeping your judgement in charge of the output. Pick the one that matches the job in front of you, adapt the example prompt, and run it on something real.

Where RTCF fits

RTCF — Role · Task · Context · Format — is how you structure any prompt, and it’s covered in the in-browser RTCF toolkit. These seven techniques are patterns you apply with it: they tell you what shape of conversation to run, while RTCF makes sure each prompt inside that conversation is well-formed. So you might use RTCF to set a single prompt up crisply, and use Role Play to decide you want a coached rehearsal rather than a one-shot answer. Think of RTCF as the grammar and these seven as the moves.

Why these seven

These aren’t random AI tricks. They’re seven prompting patterns chosen because they build the judgement AI can’t supply on its own:

  1. Reverse Prompting → comprehensive scoping and requirement gathering
  2. Pros and Cons → analytical, evidence-based decision-making
  3. Stepwise Chain of Thought → deliberate, defensible process
  4. Role Play → communication and interpersonal skill under pressure
  5. Debating → strategic and ethical reasoning across competing views
  6. Practice and Self-Testing → low-stakes rehearsal with immediate feedback
  7. The Expert Panel → multi-perspective analysis and synthesis

Each is easy to adapt to your own discipline. Let’s work through them with ready-to-use examples.

Technique 1: Reverse Prompting (for Scoping and Policy Design)

What It Does

Instead of jumping straight to a solution, the AI asks you questions to help scope out all the requirements, considerations, and potential issues. This mirrors real professional work, where defining the problem properly is half the battle.

Why It Works Across Disciplines

Complex problem scoping is fundamental to every business discipline. Whether you’re designing a policy, a strategy, a system, or a solution, you have to think through multiple stakeholders, constraints, legal and ethical implications, and trade-offs. This technique forces you past the obvious surface issues and into the full complexity before you start solving.

Discipline-specific contexts: - HR: Policy and program design with stakeholder, legal, operational, and fairness considerations - Business/Marketing: Strategy development with market, competitive, financial, and capability considerations - Management: Organisational change with stakeholder, cultural, operational, and risk considerations - Supply Chain: Network design with cost, resilience, sustainability, and operational considerations - Accounting/Finance: Audit or valuation scope with compliance, materiality, and stakeholder considerations - Economics: Policy design with trade-offs, stakeholder impacts, and evidence considerations

Prompt (ready to use)

I need to draft a new "Flexible Work Arrangement" policy for a 500-employee
company that has both office-based and remote staff. I need to make sure I
cover all the key areas.

Your task: Ask me a series of yes/no or short-answer questions to help me
clarify all the requirements, considerations, and potential pitfalls for
this policy.

Ask one question at a time. Wait for my response before asking the next
question. Continue until you've helped me think through at least 10
different aspects of this policy.

Begin with your first question.

What Happens

The AI will start asking questions like: - “Will all roles be eligible for flexible arrangements, or will there be specific criteria?” - “Have you considered how this will affect team collaboration and meeting schedules?” - “What equipment or technology support will the company provide for remote workers?” - “How will you handle performance measurement for remote versus office staff?” - “Are there legal or insurance implications you need to address?”

You have to think through each question before responding. By the end, you’ve been guided to consider fairness, logistics, legal compliance, technology needs, and cultural impact — all before drafting a single policy sentence.

Technique 2: Pros and Cons (for Decision Making)

What It Does

The AI systematically analyses multiple approaches to a business problem, evaluating each option against specific criteria. This develops the ability to make justified recommendations rather than just stating preferences.

Why It Works Across Disciplines

Every business professional has to evaluate competing strategies and make balanced, evidence-based recommendations. This technique builds the muscle for weighing trade-offs, considering multiple stakeholder perspectives, and justifying strategic choices.

Prompt (ready to use)

What are the main models for conducting performance reviews in a hybrid
workforce? For each model you identify, provide:

1. A brief description of how it works
2. Three key advantages
3. Three key disadvantages

Evaluate each model specifically in terms of:
- Fairness and perceived equity
- Administrative overhead and manager workload
- Impact on employee morale and development

Conclude with a recommendation for which model would be most appropriate
for a mid-sized tech company (300 employees, 60% hybrid, 40% fully remote)
and justify your recommendation.

What Happens

The AI will identify 3-4 performance review models (e.g., annual reviews, continuous feedback, 360-degree feedback, OKR-based systems) and analyse each systematically. You receive a structured comparison that you then have to critically evaluate.

Push back on the result

Don’t simply accept the AI’s recommendation. Force yourself to: - Challenge one of the “pros” listed — is it really an advantage in every context? - Add a disadvantage the AI missed - Argue for a different option than the one the AI recommended, using evidence - Identify which psychological, economic, or organisational theory supports or contradicts each approach

That turns the AI output from an answer into a starting point for deeper analysis.

Technique 3: Stepwise Chain of Thought (for Process Management)

What It Does

The AI walks through a complex business process one step at a time, pausing after each step until you signal you’re ready to continue. It slows down high-stakes processes that are easy to rush.

Why It Works Across Disciplines

Many professional processes are legally, ethically, or operationally sensitive. You need to understand why each step exists and what considerations apply at each stage. This technique forces deliberate, sequential thinking and real engagement with the process logic.

Prompt (ready to use)

I am learning how to properly conduct a disciplinary action process for an
employee with a pattern of repeated lateness.

Your task: Walk me through the entire disciplinary process, starting from
the very first informal conversation. For each step, tell me:

1. What action to take
2. What to document
3. What legal or procedural consideration is important at this stage

After you explain each step, STOP and wait for me to type "next" before
moving to the next step. Do not provide the entire process at once.

Begin with Step 1.

What Happens

The AI will provide the first step:

Step 1: Informal Verbal Warning

Action: Meet privately with the employee to discuss the lateness pattern. Use a calm, non-accusatory tone. Ask if there are any circumstances contributing to the issue (e.g., transport problems, personal issues, health concerns).

Documentation: Make a brief file note recording the date, what was discussed, and the employee’s response. This is informal, so no formal warning letter yet.

Legal/Procedural Consideration: At this stage, you’re gathering information and providing an opportunity for the employee to explain. Under principles of procedural fairness, you must give them a chance to be heard before escalating.

Type “next” when you’re ready for Step 2.

You have to actively engage with each step before progressing. It stops you skipping to “just fire them” without understanding the required process.

Technique 4: Role Play (for Skill Building)

What It Does

The AI adopts a specific persona and runs a realistic conversation with you. Think of it as a flight simulator for the parts of your job where you can’t afford to get it wrong the first time.

Why It Works Across Disciplines

Business work is fundamentally about interaction — with clients, stakeholders, teams, and colleagues. You can memorise the theory, but until you’ve practised actually having a difficult conversation where the other person pushes back, gets emotional, or raises objections, you won’t develop genuine interpersonal competence.

Prompt (ready to use)

You are an experienced and empathetic HR Business Partner. I am a new,
nervous manager who needs to put one of my team members on a Performance
Improvement Plan (PIP).

Your goal is to coach me through preparing for and conducting this meeting
successfully.

Guide me through this step by step:
1. First, ask me why the employee is being put on a PIP
2. Wait for my answer
3. If my answer is vague or subjective (e.g., "bad attitude"), challenge me
   gently and ask me to be more specific and behavioural
4. Once I give you a solid answer, move to the next aspect: helping me
   draft clear, measurable objectives for the PIP
5. Continue coaching me through each element of the conversation I need to
   have with the employee

Throughout this process, if I say something that could be problematic
(legally risky, unfair, too harsh, or too vague), pause and give me
feedback on how to improve it.

Begin by asking me your first question.

What Happens

The AI becomes a coach who actively guides you through the preparation. If you say “I’m putting them on a PIP because they’re lazy,” the AI will respond:

“I understand you’re frustrated, but ‘lazy’ is subjective and not something we can measure or document defensibly. Let’s try again: what specific behaviours or performance gaps have you observed? For example, are they missing deadlines, producing work below the required standard, or not completing their assigned tasks?”

This iterative coaching helps you think and speak like a professional.

Advanced Variation: The Difficult Conversation

Once you’re comfortable, flip the scenario:

You are Sarah, a high-performing marketing manager who has just been told
you're being put on a Performance Improvement Plan. You are shocked,
defensive, and angry because you believe this is unfair and politically
motivated.

I am the HR representative who has to conduct this PIP meeting with you.

Your behaviour:
- Start the conversation by immediately challenging the fairness of the PIP
- Bring up a recent successful project you led
- Hint that you believe this is retaliation for raising a complaint about
  your manager last month
- Be emotional but not abusive. Show genuine distress

I will attempt to conduct this meeting professionally. Stay in character
until I manage to de-escalate the situation and establish a constructive
dialogue.

Begin the meeting. I will speak first.

Now you’re managing a realistic, emotionally charged scenario. You practise de-escalation, empathy, procedural fairness, and documentation — all while the AI responds dynamically to what you say.

Technique 5: Debating (for Strategic and Ethical Analysis)

What It Does

The AI examines multiple perspectives on a contentious issue, argues for and against different positions, and helps you see the complexity and trade-offs in a strategic decision.

Why It Works Across Disciplines

Modern business work lives in the grey areas where competing legitimate perspectives exist. Whether you’re addressing an HR challenge, a strategy question, an operational trade-off, a financial decision, or a policy call, you need to be able to argue multiple sides of an issue before making a defensible recommendation.

Variation A: Critique and Compare

I will provide you with two different "Bring Your Own Device (BYOD)"
policies from two fictional companies.

Your task:
1. Critique the strengths and weaknesses of each policy in terms of
   security, employee privacy, and clarity
2. Tell me which policy is stronger overall and why
3. Identify one element from the weaker policy that could improve the
   stronger one

Here is Policy A: [paste policy]
Here is Policy B: [paste policy]

Begin your analysis.

Use it when: you’ve got two draft policies on the desk (or you generate them with the AI, one deliberately flawed). Identify which is stronger and justify your reasoning before you commit. It’s a fast way to build critical evaluation muscle.

Variation B: Argue Both Sides

I want you to debate the pros and cons of using AI-powered tools to scan
resumes and filter job applicants.

Structure your response in three parts:

**Part 1: The Case FOR Using AI Resume Screening**
Make the strongest possible argument in favour of this technology. Consider
efficiency, consistency, potential to reduce human bias, cost savings, and
scalability.

**Part 2: The Case AGAINST Using AI Resume Screening**
Make the strongest possible argument against this technology. Consider
algorithmic bias, legal risk, lack of contextual understanding, potential
discrimination, and dehumanization of the recruitment process.

**Part 3: Critical Synthesis**
Critique both arguments you just made. What did each side get right? What
did each side oversimplify or ignore? Provide a balanced recommendation
with conditions (e.g., "Use AI screening only if X, Y, and Z safeguards
are in place").

What happens: The AI builds sophisticated arguments for both positions, then a nuanced synthesis. You can’t just pick a side — you have to understand the legitimate concerns and benefits on both.

Variation C: Multi-Stakeholder Debate

This is the most sophisticated version:

You will simulate a leadership debate on whether to implement a "mandatory
return to office" policy requiring all employees to work in-office four
days per week.

Create three distinct personas and have them debate this issue:

**Persona 1: The CFO (Chief Financial Officer)**
Focus on: Real estate costs, productivity metrics, and financial efficiency

**Persona 2: The CHRO (Chief HR Officer)**
Focus on: Employee morale, retention, recruitment competitiveness, and
diversity/equity/inclusion implications

**Persona 3: The COO (Chief Operating Officer)**
Focus on: Team collaboration, operational consistency, training
effectiveness, and company culture

Have each persona make their opening argument (3-4 sentences each). Then
have them respond to each other's points. Continue the debate through three
rounds until they need to make a recommendation to the CEO.

After the debate, summarise the key tension points and what a compromise
policy might look like.

Begin the debate.

What happens: The AI creates a realistic executive debate where each stakeholder has legitimate but competing priorities. You see that there’s rarely a perfect answer, only trade-offs and compromise.

Push it further

After reading the debate, push yourself to: 1. Identify which stakeholder made the strongest argument, and why 2. Propose a policy that addresses the COO’s concerns while minimising the CHRO’s risks 3. Explain what data or evidence would help resolve the CFO’s questions 4. Name the organisational or economic theory that best explains the tension between these perspectives

Try this

Run the two-sided debate before your next high-stakes recommendation — a board paper, an investment memo, client advice. If you can’t articulate the strongest case against your own position, you’re not ready to defend it.

Technique 6: Practice and Self-Testing (for Rehearsal and Confidence)

What It Does

The AI generates scenarios you can use to rehearse a judgement call, prep for a difficult conversation or negotiation, or self-check your understanding of a domain before a high-stakes decision. It gives you low-stakes repetition and immediate feedback — the reps you can’t safely get on the job.

Why It Works

Many professional judgement calls need practice you can’t get for real without risk: advising on a legal point, running a sensitive conversation, reading a tricky market signal. You need more reps than the day-to-day provides, and a colleague’s time to walk you through them is scarce. AI gives you a simulator — infinite scenarios, instant generation, immediate feedback, and difficulty that adapts to how you’re doing.

Use Case 1: Self-check your understanding of a domain

Prompt:

I need to advise on Australian employment law and I want to check my own
understanding before I do. I need to practice identifying which legislation
applies in different workplace scenarios.

Generate 10 short scenario descriptions (2-3 sentences each) where I need
to identify:
1. Which legislation is relevant (e.g., Fair Work Act, WHS Act,
   Anti-Discrimination Act)
2. What the employer's legal obligation is
3. What risk exists if the employer doesn't comply

After I answer each one, tell me if I'm correct and explain why.

Begin with Scenario 1.

What happens: The AI creates scenario after scenario. You test yourself, get immediate feedback, and keep going until you’re confident. Unlike a textbook with a handful of practice problems, this gives you unlimited reps.

Key feature: the AI adapts. If you get several wrong, ask it to simplify; if you’re nailing the basics, ask it to make them more complex.

Use Case 2: Application practice

Prompt:

I'm learning to identify different types of workplace conflict (task
conflict, relationship conflict, process conflict).

Create 8 brief workplace scenarios. For each one, I'll identify the type
of conflict and suggest an appropriate intervention approach.

After each scenario, provide feedback on my classification and intervention
suggestion. If I'm wrong, explain why and what I missed.

Make the scenarios progressively more complex. Start easy, end with
ambiguous situations where multiple interpretations are valid.

Begin with Scenario 1.

What happens: Progressive difficulty builds confidence and competence. You move from obvious cases to nuanced judgement calls — exactly the territory where a wrong read is costly.

Use Case 3: Rehearse a difficult conversation

Prompt:

I have to run a difficult salary negotiation with a key supplier next
week. I want to rehearse before I'm in the room.

Generate five progressively harder negotiation scenarios I might face:
each one a short situation (3-4 sentences) with a specific demand or
objection from the supplier. After each scenario:
1. Let me write my response
2. Give me feedback on what I did well and what to improve
3. Tell me what a skilled negotiator would do differently

Topics to cover: anchoring, trade-offs, breaking deadlocks, handling
"take it or leave it" pressure, protecting the relationship.

Begin with Scenario 1.

What happens: You walk into the real conversation having already handled the awkward moments. Each rep sharpens your phrasing and your fallbacks, so the first time you hear “take it or leave it” isn’t live.

Use Case 4: Close your own knowledge gaps

Prompt:

I need to make a recommendation on procedural fairness in a workplace
investigation, and I'm not confident I've got the legal requirements
straight. I think I might be confusing "being fair" with the specific
tests.

Create a 10-question diagnostic that tests whether I actually understand:
1. The right to be informed of allegations
2. The right to respond to allegations
3. The requirement for impartial decision-making
4. The requirement to consider the response before deciding
5. The right to representation/support

For each question, use a scenario. After each answer, give me feedback
explaining the principle — not just whether I'm right.

Generate the diagnostic.

What you get: A focused self-check that surfaces the edges of your understanding before you advise or decide — so the gap shows up here, in private, not in front of a stakeholder.

Try this

Run it solo before a big decision, or with a colleague: each of you generates five scenarios and tests the other, then compare answers and reasoning. The peer version catches the blind spots a solo run misses.

Rehearsal, not the real thing

This technique is for practice, not for the decision itself.

Why that distinction matters: - You can regenerate until you get scenarios you already know the answer to — so don’t let an easy run fool you into overconfidence - AI scenarios lack the rigour of a real, messy situation with real consequences - The point is to struggle and make mistakes here, safely, so you make fewer of them when it counts

The value: unlimited, personalised rehearsal that builds confidence and competence before the high-stakes moment.

Variation: Adaptive difficulty

Advanced prompt:

I want to practice identifying workplace bullying vs. performance management
vs. legitimate reasonable management action.

Create scenarios for me to classify. After each one:
- If I get it right, make the next scenario more nuanced and ambiguous
- If I get it wrong, make the next scenario clearer and more obvious
- Continue until I can correctly identify 5 difficult/ambiguous cases in a row

Track my progress and tell me when I've achieved mastery.

Begin.

This creates a personalised path — the AI adjusts the difficulty based on how you’re performing.

Self-directed progression

You can also drive the difficulty yourself through smart prompting:

Prompt:

I want to practice identifying workplace bullying vs. legitimate performance
management vs. reasonable management action.

Generate scenarios for me to classify. After I answer each one, tell me if
I'm correct.

Track my performance. When I get 8 out of 10 correct, automatically make
the next batch of scenarios more complex and ambiguous. When I get fewer
than 5 out of 10 correct, make the next batch clearer and more obvious.

Continue until I can correctly identify 5 difficult, ambiguous cases in a row.

Begin with Scenario 1 at medium difficulty.

What this achieves: - Difficulty that progresses with you - Mastery-based advancement - Confidence built through appropriate challenge - No technical setup — just good prompting

Why this matters

Traditional rehearsal is limited by finite scenarios, the cost of someone’s time to walk you through them, and no feedback until after the fact. AI removes those constraints — infinite scenarios, instant generation, immediate feedback, adaptive difficulty. The result: you rehearse until you’re confident, not until you run out of someone’s patience.

Technique 7: The Expert Panel (for Multi-Perspective Analysis)

What It Does

You consult multiple AI “experts”, each with a different professional perspective on the same problem, then synthesise their competing advice into a recommendation.

Why It Works

Professional problems rarely have a single “correct” answer. They sit at the intersection of psychology, law, business strategy, finance, and ethics. This technique forces you to consider legitimate but competing perspectives and make informed choices about priorities and trade-offs.

Prompt (ready to use)

I need to address high turnover in our sales department (35% annual turnover;
industry average is 18%). This is affecting team morale, increasing
recruitment costs, and impacting customer relationships.

Create three expert personas who will each analyse this problem from their
professional perspective:

**Expert 1: Organisational Psychologist**
Focus on: Employee motivation, job satisfaction, workplace culture, team
dynamics, psychological contracts

**Expert 2: Compensation & Benefits Specialist**
Focus on: Pay equity, market competitiveness, incentive structures, total
rewards, cost-benefit analysis

**Expert 3: Talent Management Strategist**
Focus on: Career development, succession planning, retention programs,
employee value proposition, organisational capability

Have each expert independently provide:
1. Their diagnosis of why turnover is high (from their perspective)
2. Their recommended intervention strategy
3. Potential risks or limitations of their recommended approach
4. What data they would need to validate their hypothesis

After all three experts have provided their analysis, I will synthesize
their recommendations and develop my own strategic recommendation.

Begin with Expert 1: Organisational Psychologist.

What Happens

The AI creates three distinct voices:

Organisational Psychologist: > “The 35% turnover suggests a breakdown in psychological contract or poor person-organisation fit. Sales roles are inherently stressful, but this level suggests systemic issues, possibly unrealistic targets, poor management relationships, or lack of autonomy. I recommend: (1) Conduct stay/exit interview analysis focusing on psychological factors, (2) Assess manager-employee relationship quality, (3) Review goal-setting processes…”

Compensation Specialist: > “At 35% turnover, you’re likely seeing pay inequity or uncompetitive base/commission structures. Sales talent is mobile and market-driven. I recommend: (1) Conduct compensation benchmarking against competitors, (2) Analyse pay dispersion within the team, (3) Review commission structures for fairness and achievability…”

Talent Management Strategist: > “High turnover in sales suggests limited growth opportunities or unclear career paths. Top performers leave when they plateau. I recommend: (1) Map career progression pathways, (2) Implement mentoring for high-potentials, (3) Create lateral development opportunities…”

Turn it into a decision

After receiving all three perspectives, push yourself to:

  1. Compare and contrast:
    • Where do the experts agree?
    • Where do they conflict?
    • Which diagnosis seems most plausible given the evidence?
  2. Synthesize:
    • “If I could only implement one intervention, which would have the most impact?”
    • “How could I combine elements from multiple recommendations?”
    • “What’s the minimum viable intervention that addresses multiple root causes?”
  3. Justify with theory:
    • “Which theory best explains my chosen approach?” (e.g., Herzberg’s two-factor theory, social exchange theory, expectancy theory)
    • “What evidence would I need to collect to validate my choice?”
  4. Acknowledge trade-offs:
    • “What am I deprioritising by choosing this approach?”
    • “What are the risks if my diagnosis is wrong?”

Variations for Different Topics

Variation 1: Policy Design

Three experts debate a proposed "Bring Your Own Device" policy:
- IT Security Officer (focus: data protection, cybersecurity)
- HR Manager (focus: employee experience, equity, privacy)
- CFO (focus: cost savings, business case, ROI)

Each argues for their priorities. Your job is to design a policy that
balances all three perspectives.

Variation 2: Ethical Dilemma

Three experts analyse whether to implement mandatory return-to-office:
- Employee Relations Specialist (focus: morale, retention, fairness)
- Operations Manager (focus: productivity, collaboration, efficiency)
- Legal Counsel (focus: contractual obligations, discrimination risk, WHS)

Your job is to recommend a policy that is ethical, legal, and operationally
sound.

Variation 3: Change Management

Three experts advise on implementing AI resume screening:
- Diversity & Inclusion Officer (focus: algorithmic bias, equity)
- Recruitment Lead (focus: efficiency, candidate experience)
- Risk & Compliance Manager (focus: legal compliance, audit trails)

Your job is to design an implementation plan that addresses all concerns.

Try this

After running an expert panel on a real decision, write yourself a one-page synthesis: where the experts agree, where they conflict, your recommendation, the theory and evidence behind it, and the risk if your read is wrong. That one page is what turns three opinions into a decision you can defend.

Why This Is Powerful

Most advice presents one “correct” approach. Professional practice demands navigating competing legitimate perspectives.

This technique teaches: - No single expert has the complete answer - Good business decisions balance multiple priorities and perspectives - Strategic thinking means choosing which perspective to prioritise when - Justification matters more than the specific choice - Real problems require synthesis, not just analysis

Combining with Other Techniques

The Expert Panel works brilliantly with:

Pros and Cons (Technique 2): > “After consulting the expert panel, use the Pros and Cons technique to evaluate each expert’s recommended intervention systematically.”

Debating (Technique 5): > “Have the three experts debate each other’s recommendations. Then you arbitrate and make the final decision.”

Role Play (Technique 4): > “After developing your strategy based on expert consultation, role-play presenting it to a skeptical stakeholder (CFO, union rep, CEO) and defend your choices.”

How to Choose Which Technique to Use

Match the technique to the job in front of you:

If you want to… Use this technique
Define a complex problem or policy comprehensively Reverse Prompting
Evaluate competing options and justify a choice Pros and Cons
Follow a legally/ethically sensitive process correctly Stepwise Chain of Thought
Practise a difficult conversation or negotiation Role Play
Understand multiple perspectives and strategic trade-offs Debating
Rehearse a judgement call and self-check your understanding Practice and Self-Testing
Synthesise expert advice from different disciplines or roles The Expert Panel

Combining Techniques

The real power comes from combining these techniques. For example, a complete work sequence:

  1. Use Reverse Prompting to scope out all the requirements for a new parental leave policy
  2. Use Pros and Cons to evaluate three different policy models
  3. Draft your chosen policy
  4. Use Role Play to practise explaining the policy to a skeptical manager
  5. Use Debating to analyse potential criticisms from different stakeholders

This sequence takes you through analysis, decision-making, communication, and critical reflection — a complete professional process.
