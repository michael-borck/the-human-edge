05
What AI actually is

It predicts the next word.

Autocomplete, scaled up. Trained on billions of pages, it learned what tends to follow what. The result looks like understanding. It's pattern-matching.

06
The one mental model to keep

A convincing average.

Fluent, plausible, usually about-right. But "about right" and "exactly right" are not the same — and knowing which you need is the whole game.

07
Once you see it as an averaging machine

Brilliant here.
Quietly wrong there.

BRILLIANT
Drafting, summarising, formatting, brainstorming, explaining, finding patterns — where "the plausible version" is the answer.
UNRELIABLE
Precise facts, figures, citations, multi-step logic, your context, ethics — where there's one right answer it can't verify.
08
Not a bug — a property

Confident. Wrong.
Same polish.

It never says "I don't know." The fluent-but-wrong answer looks identical to the fluent-and-right one. Nothing in the tone warns you.

09
The spine of the day

The trust tool.

AVERAGE → LEAN IN
Low stakes, "about right" is fine. Let AI run; you glance.
PRECISE → HUMAN IN THE LOOP
High stakes, one right answer. A person owns the decision.

10
The trust tool, on real tasks

Average or precise?
Small or large?

Drafting an internal email. Average is fine, stakes small — lean in.
Board-paper figures. Precision required, stakes large — a human owns every number.
Summarising fifty complaints for themes. Lean in for the themes, then skim for the one complaint the AI smoothed away.

11
Talking to AI well

RTCF — two halves.

R · T · F SCAFFOLDING
Weaker & self-hosted models need it spelled out. A frontier model infers it from clear writing.
C ALWAYS MATTERS
Context is the one thing only you supply. No model manufactures your edge.
The durable skill: being clear about what you want.11
12
Before & after

Unusable. Then sendable.

Without: "Write an email about the project delay."

With RTCF: a senior PM, professional & empathetic, writes a client email explaining a 2-week delay (vendor integration; client values transparency; new date March 15), under 200 words — acknowledge → explain → new date → recommit.

Context is where your edge enters.

20
Difference 01

"Done" won't
hold still.

Ask twice, get two answers. There's no single correct output — only a spread. Stop chasing a fixed spec; decide what "good enough" looks like for this use.

21
Difference 02

The demo is a trap.

It shows the happy path. The messy real-world cases are where it falls over. Treat the demo as the start of the hard part — not the end.

22
Difference 03

The data is
the uncertainty.

You don't order it; you discover it — its gaps, biases and surprises show up only when you work with it. Run delivery as discovery, and build the learning into the plan.

23
Difference 04

Verification
is the product.

Outputs vary and can be confidently wrong. The value lives in how you check them, and where a human stays in the loop. Design the checking deliberately — make it part of what you ship.

24
Difference 05

Generic is the
baseline, not the edge.

When every rival runs the same models, the model isn't your advantage — anyone can have it. Compete on the human variation a rival's identical model can't reproduce.

25
