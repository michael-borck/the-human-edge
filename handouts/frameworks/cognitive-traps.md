# Three cognitive traps when working with AI

Evaluating individual AI outputs is one thing. But there are three broader cognitive traps worth naming, because they shape how people relate to AI across *everything* they do with it. These aren't technical problems. They're human ones.

## 1. The Gell-Mann amnesia effect

You read a news article about something you know well and spot the errors immediately – the reporter misunderstood the mechanism, conflated two concepts, or drew a conclusion the evidence doesn't support. You notice because you have the expertise to notice.

Then you turn the page to an article about a subject you know nothing about, and you read it with full trust.

That's the Gell-Mann Amnesia Effect: recognising unreliability in familiar territory, then forgetting that unreliability the moment you move to unfamiliar territory.

With AI, this trap is especially dangerous. You catch the AI's mistakes in your own field, and rightly so. But the same AI, using the same process, will generate output on topics you don't know well – and because the output is fluent, well-structured, and confident, you'll be tempted to trust it in exactly the areas where you're least equipped to check it.

This isn't a flaw in the AI. It's a flaw in how we respond to fluency. Polished language feels authoritative. The smoother the prose, the harder it is to remember that smoothness and accuracy are unrelated.

**The defence:** apply the same scepticism to AI output on unfamiliar topics that you'd apply to output on topics you know. If anything, be *more* sceptical – because you have fewer tools to detect errors.

## 2. The AI dismissal fallacy

The opposite trap is just as damaging: rejecting an argument, idea, or piece of work solely because AI was involved in producing it.

"That's just ChatGPT" is not a critique. It's a refusal to engage. The validity of an idea doesn't depend on whether a human or a machine produced it. If someone presents a well-reasoned argument and it's dismissed without engaging the reasoning, the *dismissal* is the intellectual failure – not the use of AI.

This is the genetic fallacy: judging a claim by its origin rather than its content. It shows up in workplaces where people dismiss a colleague's analysis because they know AI was involved, and in public discourse where "AI-generated" has become shorthand for "not worth reading."

The irony: people who dismiss AI-assisted work often accept ideas from books, consultants, and collaborators without demanding to know the exact cognitive process behind them. The objection isn't about quality. It's about the tool.

## 3. The sycophancy trap

This one the AI actively participates in.

LLMs tend to agree with you. They praise your ideas, validate your assumptions, and avoid telling you things you might not want to hear. Researchers call this **sycophancy** – telling you what you want to hear rather than what you need to hear. It's not a bug; it's baked into how models are trained (optimised on human feedback, and humans rate agreeable responses more highly than challenging ones).

This matters for everything from writing to analysis to decisions. If the AI tells you your first draft is excellent, your argument is compelling, and your plan has no weaknesses, you stop iterating. You stop improving because the AI told you there's nothing to improve.

**The defence: prompt past it.** Don't ask "what do you think?" Ask "what are the three weakest points in this argument?" or "play devil's advocate and tell me why this plan will fail." Be direct: "Don't flatter me. I need honest, critical feedback. Tell me what's wrong before you tell me what's right."

## The takeaway

These three traps – trusting AI more where you know less, dismissing AI output without engaging it, and accepting AI flattery as honest feedback – are not technical problems. They're human ones. Awareness is the first defence.
